Writing Dialogue for AI Video: Mastering the 95-Character Limit

Constraints breed creativity. Here's how to write powerful dialogue that works within Veo 3's technical limits.

Understanding the 95-Character Limit

Veo 3's 95-character dialogue limit isn't arbitrary—it's designed to ensure clear, natural-sounding speech synthesis. Longer dialogue can result in rushed speech, unnatural pacing, or complete generation failures.

But here's the thing: 95 characters is more than you think. This entire sentence is 58 characters. You have room to work with—you just need to use it wisely.

Character Count Examples

  • "Hello." = 6 characters
  • "I need to talk to you about something." = 40 characters
  • "We need to leave right now before they find us here." = 53 characters
  • "I've been waiting my whole life for this moment, and I'm not going to let it slip away." = 90 characters

Technique 1: Condensation

The most powerful technique is saying the same thing with fewer words. This isn't about dumbing down your dialogue—it's about precision.

Before: 142 characters (too long)

"I've been thinking about what you said yesterday, and I've come to the conclusion that you were absolutely right about everything."

After: 47 characters (perfect)

"I thought about it. You were right all along."

Condensation Strategies:

  • Remove filler words: "I think that maybe" → "Maybe"
  • Use contractions: "I am going to" → "I'm gonna"
  • Cut redundancy: "absolutely certain" → "certain"
  • Replace phrases with words: "at this point in time" → "now"
  • Trust subtext: Let visuals convey what words don't need to

Technique 2: Strategic Splitting

When condensation isn't enough, split the dialogue across multiple scenes. This isn't a compromise—it's an opportunity for better pacing and visual variety.

Original: 156 characters

"I found the documents you were looking for. They prove everything we suspected. The company has been lying to everyone from the very beginning."

Split into 3 scenes

Scene 1 (43 chars): "I found the documents you were looking for."

Scene 2 (36 chars): "They prove everything we suspected."

Scene 3 (52 chars): "The company's been lying to everyone from the start."

Where to Split:

  • Natural pauses: After periods, at commas, before "and" or "but"
  • Emotional beats: Let important statements stand alone
  • Reaction points: Split where another character would naturally react
  • Topic changes: New subject = new scene

Technique 3: Show, Don't Tell

The best dialogue often isn't dialogue at all. Let visuals carry the emotional weight.

Telling (112 characters)

"I'm so incredibly nervous about this interview. My hands are shaking and I can barely breathe right now."

Showing (visual + short dialogue)

Visual prompt: "Close-up of hands trembling slightly, straightening a tie. Quick breath. Eyes dart to the door."

Dialogue (24 chars): "Okay. You've got this."

The visual conveys nervousness far more effectively than words ever could. The short self-encouragement adds character without exposition.

Technique 4: Subtext and Implication

Great dialogue says one thing and means another. This naturally leads to shorter lines because you're not explaining everything.

On-the-nose (98 characters)

"I'm really angry at you for what you did, and I don't think I can ever forgive you for betraying me."

Subtext (28 characters)

"I hope it was worth it."

The second version is more powerful because it forces the audience to understand the emotion through context and delivery, not explanation.

Practical Examples by Genre

Drama

Too long (108): "After everything we've been through together, I never thought it would end like this between us."

Condensed (45): "After everything... this is how it ends?"

Comedy

Too long (115): "I specifically told you not to touch anything, and somehow you managed to break the one thing that was irreplaceable."

Condensed (62): "I said don't touch anything. You broke the ONE irreplaceable thing."

Thriller

Too long (98): "Someone has been following me for the past three days, and I think they know where I live now."

Condensed (51): "Someone's been following me. They know where I live."

Romance

Too long (102): "I've spent so many years trying to find someone who understands me, and then you just appeared in my life."

Condensed (52): "I spent years looking for someone like you. Then... you."

Word Replacement Cheat Sheet

Instead of... Use... Saves
"I am going to" "I'll" 10 chars
"Do you not" "Don't you" 1 char
"at this moment" "now" 11 chars
"in order to" "to" 9 chars
"the reason is because" "because" 14 chars
"I believe that" "I think" 7 chars
"in spite of the fact that" "although" 17 chars
"a large number of" "many" 13 chars

Common Mistakes

  1. Counting wrong: Remember, spaces and punctuation count! "Hello, world!" is 13 characters, not 10.
  2. Losing meaning: Condensation should preserve intent. "I love you more than anything" shouldn't become "Love ya."
  3. Unnatural speech: Real people use contractions and fragments. "I am not going to do that" sounds robotic; "I'm not doing that" sounds human.
  4. Over-splitting: Not every line needs its own scene. Sometimes a 60-character line is perfect as-is.
  5. Forgetting emotion: Short doesn't mean flat. "No." can be devastating with the right visual context.
